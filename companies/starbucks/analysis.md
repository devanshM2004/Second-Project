# Starbucks Analysis

## Overview

This case study uses a simplified financial model to show how Starbucks' business activity can be translated into the three core financial statements: the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement.

The purpose of this model is educational. It is designed to strengthen understanding of financial statement structure, statement linkage, and how operating assumptions affect business outcomes.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Revenue growth drives profitability

In this model, Starbucks generates revenue of $100.00. After accounting for cost of goods sold, operating expenses, depreciation, interest expense, and taxes, the business produces net income of $20.25.

This shows how sales growth alone is not enough. Profitability depends on whether costs are controlled as revenue increases.

### 2. Margins matter

The model highlights the importance of gross profit and operating income. Even with strong revenue, high operating expenses can significantly reduce final profitability.

This is especially relevant for a business like Starbucks, where store operations, labor, rent, and brand investment all affect margins.

### 3. Cash flow tells a different story than net income

Although the model shows net income of $20.25, the cash flow statement explains how actual cash changes after adjusting for non-cash expenses, working capital movements, investing activity, and financing activity.

This reinforces an important finance concept: a company can report profit, but cash generation still depends on how the business is managed operationally and financially.

### 4. The balance sheet reflects the end result

The balance sheet shows what the company owns, what it owes, and the residual value held by equity. In this model, ending cash from the cash flow statement appears as cash on the balance sheet, helping connect the statements together.

## Limitations

This model is simplified and built for learning purposes. It is not intended to match Starbucks' exact reported financials. Future versions can improve realism by using actual annual report data and tighter statement linkage.
