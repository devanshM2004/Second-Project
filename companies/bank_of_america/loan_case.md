# Bank of America – Consumer Loan Case Studies

## Purpose

This document applies the 5 Cs of Credit framework to three borrower scenarios with different risk profiles. Each scenario walks through the underwriting logic step by step, as a credit analyst would document it in a credit memo.

---

## Scenario A – Borderline Borrower (Moderate Risk)

### Borrower Profile

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Annual Gross Income | $68,000 |
| Monthly Gross Income | $5,667 |
| Employment | Full-time, salaried — 3 years at current employer |
| Credit Score (FICO) | 685 |
| Loan Purpose | Home improvement |
| Loan Requested | $22,000 |
| Loan Term | 48 months |
| Estimated Interest Rate | 10.5% APR |
| Estimated Monthly Payment | ~$563 |
| Cash Savings | $5,500 |

---

### Step 1: Capacity – Debt-to-Income (DTI) Analysis

**Existing Monthly Debt Obligations:**

| Obligation | Monthly Payment |
|---|---|
| Auto loan | $420 |
| Student loan | $215 |
| Credit card minimums | $175 |
| **Total (existing debt)** | **$810** |

**Post-Loan DTI Calculation:**

```
DTI = (Existing debt + New payment) / Gross monthly income
DTI = ($810 + $563) / $5,667
DTI = $1,373 / $5,667
DTI = 24.2%
```

Guideline: DTI ≤ 43% → **Within acceptable range ✓**

The borrower retains meaningful cash flow after all debt obligations.

---

### Step 2: Character – Credit Profile

- FICO score: **685** (Near-prime)
- No reported delinquencies in the past 24 months
- 4 open accounts in good standing
- 1 late payment on credit card 30 months ago

Assessment: Acceptable. Not a prime borrower, but no recent derogatory marks. The near-prime score will influence the rate offered, not the approval decision.

---

### Step 3: Capital – Reserves

- Cash savings: $5,500
- Months of payment coverage: $5,500 / $563 = **9.8 months**

Guideline: ≥ 2–3 months of reserves → **Passes ✓**

The borrower has a sufficient liquidity buffer to cover payments through a period of income disruption.

---

### Step 4: Collateral

This is an **unsecured personal loan** — no collateral backing.

Implication: The lender bears full credit risk in the event of default. This is priced into the interest rate: 10.5% APR reflects both the borrower's near-prime credit profile and the absence of collateral. A secured home equity loan for the same purpose would typically carry a rate of 6–8% APR.

---

### Step 5: Conditions

- Loan purpose: Home improvement (productive use — positive signal)
- Employment: Stable, salaried, 3 years tenure
- Economic environment: Stable, no sector-specific risk concerns

---

### Step 6: Risk Summary

| Factor | Detail | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| DTI (24.2%) | Well below 43% threshold | Positive |
| Credit Score (685) | Near-prime; no recent derogatory marks | Moderate |
| Reserves (9.8 months) | Exceeds minimum guideline | Positive |
| Collateral | None — unsecured loan | Negative |
| Loan Purpose | Home improvement — productive use | Positive |
| Employment | Stable, salaried | Positive |

**Overall Risk Rating:** Moderate — Acceptable

---

### Step 7: Credit Decision

**Decision: APPROVE**

- Loan Amount: $22,000
- Term: 48 months
- Rate: 10.5% APR (risk-adjusted for near-prime score and unsecured structure)
- Monthly Payment: ~$563

**Rationale:** The borrower demonstrates adequate repayment capacity (DTI 24.2%), a clean recent credit history, and sufficient reserves. The near-prime FICO score is reflected in the above-market rate rather than a denial. No material red flags in character, conditions, or purpose.

## Scenario B – Strong Borrower (Low Risk)

### Borrower Profile

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Annual Gross Income | $105,000 |
| Monthly Gross Income | $8,750 |
| Employment | Full-time, salaried — 6 years at current employer |
| Credit Score (FICO) | 762 |
| Loan Requested | $25,000 personal loan |
| Loan Term | 48 months |
| Estimated Interest Rate | 7.9% APR |
| Estimated Monthly Payment | ~$609 |
| Cash Savings | $28,000 |

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Existing monthly debt | Auto loan $520 + credit card $100 = $620 | $620/month |
| Post-loan DTI | ($620 + $609) / $8,750 | **14.0%** |
| Reserve coverage | $28,000 / $609 | **45.9 months** |

### Credit Decision: **APPROVE — Prime Terms**

DTI of 14.0% is well below threshold. Prime FICO score (762), strong reserves, and long employment tenure all signal low default probability. The borrower qualifies for a preferred rate of ~7.9% APR. Straightforward approval with no conditions required.

**Key insight:** This borrower is low risk not because of income alone, but because leverage is low and reserves are strong relative to obligations.

## Scenario C – High-Risk Borrower (Decline)

### Borrower Profile

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Annual Gross Income | $41,000 |
| Monthly Gross Income | $3,417 |
| Employment | Part-time, hourly — 8 months at current employer |
| Credit Score (FICO) | 592 |
| Loan Requested | $18,000 personal loan |
| Loan Term | 48 months |
| Estimated Monthly Payment | ~$490 |
| Cash Savings | $1,200 |

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Existing monthly debt | Auto loan $480 + credit card minimums $230 + medical collection $440 = $1,150 | $1,150/month |
| Post-loan DTI | ($1,150 + $490) / $3,417 | **48.0%** |
| Reserve coverage | $1,200 / $490 | **2.4 months** |

### Credit Decision: **DECLINE**

Post-loan DTI of 48.0% exceeds the 43% maximum threshold. The subprime FICO score (592) reflects an active collection account and multiple prior delinquencies, indicating an elevated probability of default. Reserve coverage barely meets the minimum, leaving no meaningful buffer.

**Key insight:** This is not just an income problem — a borrower with the same income but lower existing obligations could qualify. The issue is the combination of high leverage, poor credit history, and thin reserves.

**Possible path forward for the borrower:**
- Pay down existing credit card balances to reduce monthly obligations
- Resolve the collection account
- Return in 12–18 months with an improved credit profile
- Request a smaller loan amount ($8,000–$10,000) to bring post-loan DTI below 43%

## Scenario Comparison

| | Scenario A | Scenario B | Scenario C |
|---|---|---|---|
| FICO Score | 685 | 762 | 592 |
| Post-Loan DTI | 24.2% | 14.0% | 48.0% |
| Reserve Coverage | 9.8 months | 45.9 months | 2.4 months |
| Collateral | None | None | None |
| Decision | Approve | Approve (Prime) | Decline |
| Rate | 10.5% APR | 7.9% APR | N/A |

The comparison illustrates how the same loan amount can have three very different outcomes based on the full credit picture — not just income level.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **DTI is the primary gating metric** in consumer underwriting — calculate it first
- **Credit score affects rate, not just approval** — near-prime borrowers pay a risk premium
- **Unsecured loans carry higher rates** because the lender has no asset to recover in default
- **Thin reserves are a warning sign** even when current income looks adequate
- **Declines should include a rationale and a path forward** — this is standard practice in consumer lending and reflects good credit culture
